## Working papers
[What Happens in Paris, Does Not Stay in Paris: Trade Fairs and Search and Matching Frictions]([https://www.google.com](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1D3D2l7P2MxA3f6qm_hgb5Z1X0eFGGLQB/view))  
with [Gabor Bekes](https://gaborbekes.com/) and [Claudia Steinwender](https://sites.google.com/site/clsteinwender/)  

Search and matching frictions prevent firms from forming international trade linkages. Despite trade fairs being a common and often subsidized tool to overcome these frictions, we lack causal evidence on how they facilitate link formation.  We exploit a unique feature of Hungarian firms' participation in the 1900 Paris World Exhibition,  where a trial exhibition revealed firms’ ex-ante export potential category to develop a novel bounding strategy that compares treated firms to control groups from “above” and “below” in export potential. To implement our empirical strategy, we constructed a novel panel dataset of approximately 3,600 Hungarian manufacturing firms for the 1896–1906 period by digitizing, parsing and linking over 12,000 records across eleven historical sources, including exhibition catalogs, government surveys, commercial directories,  official gazettes and patenting directories. We find that participation increases export probability by 4-10 percentage points, patenting probability by 4-6 percentage points and employment by 16–23\% over eight years. Effects are larger when firms face fewer domestic competitors and more potential international buyers. This highlights both matching benefits and congestion effects when search and matching frictions are reduced.

## Work in progress

# Matching Across Borders: Buyer Directories and Search Frictions in the First Globalization  
Using a historical quasi-experiment from late nineteenth-century Austria-Hungary, I show that
published information about foreign buyers increases the probability of exporting to a destination
by 5–14%, identifying buyer information as a key channel through which export promotion raises
trade.

# Foreign Occupation and National Irredentism: Evidence from Hungary  
with [Ricardo Pique](https://sites.google.com/site/piquericardo/home)  

We examine the impact of military occupation by a rival neighbor on support for nationalist and irredentist movements. Following WWI, Hungary was invaded by Romanian forces. While much of the country came under Romanian control, the region east of the Tisza River endured a longer and more intense occupation. Using a spatial regression discontinuity design and settlement-level data, we first show that the eastern-bank settlements experienced higher rates of victimization during the occupation. Afterwards, these areas showed greater support for nationalist parties. As the far-right rose to prominence in the advent of WWII, these settlements were more likely to back extremist right-wing movements that prioritized Hungarian irredentist claims over the ruling traditional nationalist party. These findings highlight a strong connection between military occupation and support for extreme nationalist movements.
